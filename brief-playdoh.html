Play-Doh Was a Wallpaper Cleaner (And Wrigley Started Selling Soap)

Smithsonian Magazine · Museum of Play · Today I Found Out

In the late 1920s, Kutol Products was a struggling soap company in Cincinnati. They got a lifeline in 1933 when Kroger placed a large order for a wallpaper cleaning compound — a pliable, nontoxic dough that could lift coal soot off walls without damaging the paper. It worked well. The business stabilized. Then, after WWII, natural gas replaced coal heating and washable vinyl wallpaper replaced the older kind. The coal soot problem that Kutol's product solved simply stopped existing. The company was heading toward bankruptcy again.

Joseph McVicker, who had taken over the company, got a call from his sister-in-law Kay Zufall — a nursery school teacher who had read an article about how the cleaning compound could be used as a modeling material for children. She'd tested it with her students. They loved it. Zufall pushed McVicker to repurpose the product, came up with the name Play-Doh, and in 1956 the company launched it as a children's toy. By 1958, it was being advertised on Captain Kangaroo, Ding Dong School, and Romper Room. Sales exploded. Play-Doh went on to become one of the most iconic children's products in American history.

"The most valuable thing a company builds is often the byproduct of what they were actually trying to do."

This pattern is not unique. Wrigley's chewing gum started as a free premium included with baking powder sales — the gum was just a promotional item to move product, until customers made clear they preferred the gum to the baking powder. William Wrigley Jr. pivoted the entire company to gum. Nokia started as a Finnish paper mill in 1865, moved into rubber boots and cables in the early 20th century, and only became a telecommunications company in the 1980s when it decided to focus on what was emerging from its electronics division. Samsung began in 1938 as a trading company selling dried fish, noodles, and groceries in Korea — the electronics empire came decades later.

The Strategic Lesson

What's interesting about these stories isn't the accident itself — it's the recognition. Kutol had been sitting on a children's toy for years before anyone noticed. The compound existed; the insight that it could be repurposed is what created Play-Doh. Wrigley could have kept giving gum away as a promotional afterthought. Nokia could have stayed in paper and rubber. The skill isn't building something valuable — it's recognizing when the accidental output is worth more than the original plan, and having the willingness to abandon the plan.

In marketing and brand strategy, this plays out constantly. Products get used by audiences in ways the company never intended. Brand associations form around attributes the team didn't deliberately cultivate. Distribution channels emerge from workarounds customers invented themselves. The companies that win are often the ones watching closely enough to see what customers are actually doing with their product — and smart enough to follow that signal rather than force the original thesis. Play-Doh wasn't invented. It was recognized.
