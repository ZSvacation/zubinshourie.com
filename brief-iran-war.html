US-Israel War with Iran Enters Day 8 — Over 1,200 Dead, Strait of Hormuz Effectively Closed

Al Jazeera · The Hindu · NBC News · CNN

Eight days into coordinated US and Israeli military strikes on Iran, the conflict has crossed thresholds that analysts warned could reshape global energy markets and Middle East geopolitics for years. At least 1,332 civilians have been killed inside Iran since the campaign began on February 28, with strikes hitting oil depots, military infrastructure, and — according to reports from international observers — schools and hospitals. In Lebanon, over 200 people have been killed in concurrent Israeli operations. Six American servicemen have died, and eleven Israelis have been killed in retaliatory missile strikes.

The strategic flashpoint is the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow chokepoint through which roughly 20% of global oil and liquified natural gas moves daily. Iran's IRGC officially announced on March 2 that the strait was closed to vessels from the US, Israel, and their Western allies. While the IRGC maintains it remains technically open to neutral shipping, the practical reality is different: tanker operators are refusing to enter, insurance rates have spiked to near-uninsurable levels, and at least one bulk carrier — the Liberia-flagged Sino Ocean — broadcast its Chinese ownership explicitly to try to pass safely on March 7. The message is clear: the strait is a war zone.

"Every 10% increase in oil prices pushes global inflation up by 0.4 percentage points and reduces worldwide economic output by as much as 0.2%." — IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva

The economic shock is already materializing. Brent crude has surged from roughly $70/barrel before the conflict to over $110 as of early March — a move that translates directly into higher gas prices, elevated input costs for manufacturers, and tighter household budgets everywhere. In the US, national average gas prices crossed $3.32/gallon on March 7 with analysts forecasting $3.50 within weeks. The IMF has warned that if Brent stays at current levels, global inflation could rise by nearly a full percentage point — exactly the kind of shock that derails the rate-cut cycle central banks had been carefully engineering.

Trump's Position and Diplomatic Deadlock

President Trump approved a new $151 million arms sale to Israel during the week and publicly stated he would not negotiate without Iran's "unconditional surrender." Tehran, for its part, has threatened to extend the Hormuz closure indefinitely while retaliating with ballistic missiles against Gulf state oil infrastructure. Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE — all sitting on the wrong side of Iran's threat perimeter — are caught between American alliance obligations and existential economic exposure.

On the ground in the US, "March for Peace" demonstrations were scheduled in cities across the country on March 7. The political calculus is sharpening: energy prices are one of the most visceral economic signals ordinary Americans feel, and a prolonged conflict keeping oil above $100 is a domestic political problem regardless of foreign policy posture. How quickly this resolves — or doesn't — will define the economic backdrop for the rest of 2026.
