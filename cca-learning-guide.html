01
Foundation
Ultra-High-Net-Worth Wealth Management

CCA serves clients with $25M–$1B+ in investable assets. This is a fundamentally different world than retail banking or even high-net-worth advisory. Understanding what UHNW clients actually care about — and how wealth management at this level differs from what most people picture — is the foundation for everything else.

The Wealth Tiers

Mass Affluent
$100K–$1M in investable assets. Served by big banks, robo-advisors, retail brokerages. Cookie-cutter portfolios: 60/40 stocks and bonds.
High Net Worth (HNW)
$1M–$10M. Regional wealth managers, some wirehouses (Merrill, Morgan Stanley). Begin getting access to alternatives. Still mostly public markets.
Very High Net Worth (VHNW)
$10M–$25M. Private banking divisions of large banks. More customized planning. First real access to institutional alternatives.
Ultra-High Net Worth (UHNW)
$25M and above. CCA's market. Family office-level services: tax optimization, estate planning, philanthropy, private investments. Portfolio construction is highly customized. Alternatives can be 30–50% of portfolio. Advisors are fiduciaries with deep personal relationships.

What UHNW Clients Actually Care About

At this wealth level, the priorities shift. Generating returns is still important, but so is preserving what you have, minimizing tax drag, transferring wealth to the next generation efficiently, and deploying capital in ways that align with values. The job of an advisor like CCA is to serve all of these simultaneously.

  • Capital preservation first: Losing $20M on a bad bet is more psychologically damaging than the same loss percentage at a lower level. Downside protection matters.
  • Tax efficiency: At this income and wealth level, a bad tax structure can cost millions annually. Everything runs through a tax lens.
  • Access to private markets: UHNW clients can qualify as "qualified purchasers" and access investment vehicles not available to retail investors.
  • Estate and legacy planning: How do you transfer $100M+ to children or a foundation with minimal tax erosion? This is a major service of any UHNW advisor.
  • Concierge-level service: Clients at this level expect their advisor to know everything about their financial life, proactively identify issues, and be available.

02
Core Knowledge
Alternative Investments

Alternatives are anything outside public stocks and bonds. For UHNW clients, they can represent 30–50% of total portfolio value. CCA's core differentiator is sourcing and evaluating private market alternatives. You need to be fluent in all of the major categories.

Private Equity (PE)

Buyouts
PE firm acquires a majority stake in a mature company using a combination of equity and debt (leveraged buyout). Goal: improve operations, increase earnings, exit at a higher multiple 4–7 years later. Largest category by capital. Think: KKR, Blackstone, Apollo.
Example: KKR buys a $500M manufacturing company with $200M equity and $300M debt. Over 5 years they cut costs, grow revenue, refinance the debt. Sell for $900M. Equity return is enormous because leverage amplified the gain.
Growth Equity
Minority or majority stake in a profitable, fast-growing company that doesn't need debt to function. Less leverage than buyouts, higher growth expectations. Think: General Atlantic, Insight Partners.
Venture Capital
Early-stage equity investing. High risk, high return. Most VC-backed companies fail; returns are driven by a few large wins. CCA clients may participate as LPs in VC funds or co-invest directly in startups.

Private Credit

Private credit is lending money to companies or asset owners outside of the public bond market. It has grown dramatically since 2010 as banks pulled back from certain loan categories post-financial crisis. For UHNW portfolios, it offers bond-like income with better yields.

Direct Lending
The most common form. A fund makes a senior secured loan directly to a middle-market company. Typical yield: SOFR + 4–6%. No bank intermediary. Borrower pays more for speed and flexibility; lender earns more spread. Think: Ares Capital, Blue Owl.
Mezzanine Debt
Subordinated debt sitting between senior secured loans and equity. Higher risk than direct lending, higher yield (12–18%). Often includes equity "kickers" (warrants) that give the lender upside if the company grows.
Distressed Debt
Buying bonds or loans of companies near or in bankruptcy at discounted prices. Highly complex; requires deep credit analysis and legal expertise. Opportunity-driven, not always available.

Real Assets

Private Real Estate
Direct ownership or fund-based investment in commercial properties (office, multifamily, industrial, retail). Returns come from rental income and appreciation. Illiquid but historically stable.
Infrastructure
Airports, toll roads, pipelines, data centers, renewable energy assets. Long-duration, inflation-linked cash flows. Very attractive for UHNW portfolios seeking predictable income over decades.

Hedge Funds

Pooled investment funds that use a wide range of strategies — long/short equity, macro trading, arbitrage, quantitative — with the goal of generating returns uncorrelated to the broader market. The fee structure (historically "2 and 20" — 2% management fee, 20% of profits) has come under pressure; many now charge 1.5 and 15. Access for UHNW clients through CCA means getting into funds that may be closed to new investors or have institutional-only minimums.

Why This Matters for Your Role

When CCA evaluates an investment opportunity, they're placing it into one of these categories and then applying the right analytical framework. Part of your job may be initial screening — understanding whether a deal is direct lending, growth equity, or real assets, what the risk profile is, and whether it fits the client base. You can't evaluate what you can't classify.

03
Core Skill
Evaluating Investment Opportunities

This is the core analytical work at CCA. When they receive an investment opportunity, they need to determine quickly: is this worth our clients' capital? The framework below is what every serious alternative investment analyst uses.

Step 1: Understand What It Is

Before you can evaluate any investment, you need to be able to answer five questions in plain English: (1) What asset class is this? (2) Who is the manager and what is their track record? (3) What are the terms — return expectation, fees, minimum, lock-up? (4) What are the clients' funds being used for? (5) What is the exit mechanism?

Step 2: Key Metrics You Need to Know

IRR — Internal Rate of Return
The annualized return on invested capital, accounting for the timing of cash flows. The most common return metric in private markets.
If you invest $1M today and receive $1.8M in 5 years, the IRR is ~12.5%. If you invest the same and get $1.8M in 3 years, IRR is ~21.5%. Timing matters enormously.
MOIC — Multiple on Invested Capital
Total cash returned divided by total cash invested. A 2.0x MOIC means you doubled your money. Doesn't account for time — a 2x in 2 years is very different from a 2x in 8 years.
MOIC is commonly used alongside IRR. A fund might report "2.5x MOIC, 28% IRR" — meaning they returned 2.5x and did it quickly.
DPI — Distributed to Paid-In Capital
Cash actually returned to investors divided by capital invested. A DPI of 1.5x means investors have received 1.5x their money back in cash. Crucial distinction: unrealized portfolio value (TVPI) looks great until you actually sell.
Sharpe Ratio
Return per unit of risk (volatility). A higher Sharpe ratio means better risk-adjusted returns. Important for comparing alternatives to public market equivalents.
IRR Quick Check
Rule of 72: Divide 72 by the annual return to estimate years to double money.
10% IRR → ~7.2 years. 15% IRR → ~4.8 years. 20% IRR → ~3.6 years.

Step 3: Manager Due Diligence

In private markets, manager selection accounts for more of the variation in returns than asset class selection. A top-quartile private equity manager returns 25%+ IRR. A bottom-quartile manager in the same strategy returns 5% or less. How do you evaluate a manager?

  • Track record: Net IRR and MOIC across multiple funds, over multiple market cycles. Is it consistent or was it one good fund?
  • Team: Who are the investment professionals? Are they the same people who generated the historical returns? Key-person risk is real.
  • Sourcing edge: How does this manager find deals? Proprietary networks, industry expertise, geography? If anyone can find the same deals, the edge disappears.
  • Alignment: Is the manager's capital at risk alongside yours? GP co-investment (skin in the game) signals conviction.
  • Terms: Management fee, carry rate, preferred return ("hurdle"), LP-friendly governance. 2-and-20 is the legacy standard; negotiate toward 1.5-and-15 where possible.

Step 4: Write the Investment Memo

Every opportunity that advances past initial screening gets a written investment memo. The standard structure:

  • Executive Summary: One paragraph. What is it, what's the return, why now, what's the risk.
  • Market Context: Why is this opportunity available? What macro or structural force creates it?
  • Investment Thesis: 3–4 specific reasons to invest.
  • Risk Assessment: What could go wrong? How likely? How bad?
  • Manager/Sponsor Analysis: Who is running it and why should we trust them?
  • Portfolio Fit: Which clients does this work for? Minimum investment? Liquidity constraints?
  • Recommendation: Clear, direct. Invest / pass / watch list.

Soft Skills
UHNW Client Communication

At CCA, everything client-facing needs to communicate complexity with simplicity. UHNW clients are sophisticated, but they are not necessarily investment analysts. They're entrepreneurs, executives, and inheritors. Your job is to make the investment case clearly, acknowledge the risks honestly, and respect their time.

The Communication Pyramid

Always lead with the conclusion, then support it. UHNW clients do not want to read a lengthy analysis to find the answer on page 12. Put the recommendation in the first sentence, then explain why.

  • Lead with the "so what": "We are recommending a $2M allocation to the Blue Owl Technology Finance Fund. Here's why."
  • Then the evidence: Return profile, structural protections, manager track record, portfolio fit.
  • Then the risks: Be direct and honest. Clients trust advisors who tell them the downsides clearly.
  • Then the decision: What do you need from them to proceed?

CCA doesn't hold client money directly — it uses custodians (Charles Schwab and Goldman Sachs) to hold assets. This is standard RIA practice and provides a safety check: the custodian holds the assets, the RIA manages the strategy.

Your Domain
Marketing in Financial Services

Marketing for a registered investment advisor is heavily regulated. You can't say things like "guaranteed returns" or imply past performance predicts future results. Understanding the rules protects CCA from regulatory risk and you from making mistakes that could have serious consequences.

The Regulatory Framework

SEC Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1)
Governs how RIAs can market themselves. Key rules: no materially misleading statements, no cherry-picked performance results (must show all relevant periods), testimonials and endorsements are allowed but require specific disclosures, hypothetical performance requires extensive disclosures.
Accredited Investor vs. Qualified Purchaser
Accredited investor: $1M+ net worth or $200K+ annual income. Can access most private fund opportunities. Qualified purchaser: $5M+ in investments. Can access a wider range of private funds (3(c)(7) funds), which are what most institutional-quality alternatives use. CCA's clients are largely QPs.
Regulation D (Reg D)
Allows companies to raise money from accredited investors without registering with the SEC. Most private fund subscriptions are Reg D offerings. No general solicitation (you can't advertise a private fund opportunity publicly in most cases).

What This Means in Practice

  • Email campaigns about specific investment opportunities can only go to current clients or people who have initiated contact — not cold audiences.
  • LinkedIn content about investment themes and market views is generally fine. Specific fund recommendations are not.
  • Performance presentations must show all relevant periods, gross and net returns, and a disclosure about hypothetical performance if it's included.
  • Testimonials from clients are allowed under the new Marketing Rule, but require specific disclosures about whether the client was compensated.

High-Leverage Marketing Activities for a Boutique RIA

  • Thought leadership content: Market commentary, investment theme briefs, macroeconomic analysis. Builds credibility with potential clients and reinforces expertise with current ones.
  • Client newsletters / quarterly letters: Regular communication about markets, portfolio positioning, and opportunities. The RIA version of an investor letter.
  • Event marketing: UHNW clients are acquired through relationships. Invite-only dinners, advisory roundtables, and curated events with relevant speakers are how boutique firms grow.
  • Referral network development: CPAs, estate attorneys, and investment bankers refer UHNW clients to advisors they trust. Building and nurturing this network is marketing.
  • LinkedIn personal brand: At a firm this new, the founders' personal brands are the firm's brand. Content strategy that elevates Nathan Romano and team drives inbound leads.
Your Core Contribution Area

A new RIA with a powerful founding team but limited staff needs marketing infrastructure built from scratch: content calendar, brand voice, client communication templates, event planning, opportunity brief templates, and digital presence optimization. This is exactly the kind of work that lets you demonstrate broad marketing skills while learning institutional finance. Own the communication layer — that's where you'll add the most value.

Terminology Glossary

These are the terms you will hear in every meeting. Know them cold.

Investment Returns

IRR
Internal rate of return. Annualized return on invested capital, accounting for timing of cash flows. Primary return metric in private markets.
MOIC
Multiple on invested capital. Total cash returned ÷ cash invested. A 2.5x MOIC means $2.50 returned for every $1 invested.
DPI
Distributed to paid-in capital. Cash actually returned to investors. Unrealized value doesn't count until it's realized.
TVPI
Total value to paid-in capital. DPI + unrealized value. The total picture including paper gains.
Alpha
Returns above a benchmark. "Generating alpha" means beating the market. "Pure alpha" is return not explained by market exposure.
Beta
Sensitivity to market movements. Beta of 1 moves with the market. Beta of 0 is uncorrelated. Alternatives are chosen partly for low beta.

Fund Structure

LP / GP
Limited partner (investor in the fund) and general partner (manager who runs the fund). LPs provide capital; GPs make investment decisions and earn management fees + carry.
Carry (Carried Interest)
The GP's share of profits above the preferred return. Standard is 20%. If a fund returns $200M on $100M invested, and preferred return was met, the GP earns 20% of $100M gain = $20M.
Preferred Return (Hurdle Rate)
The minimum return LPs must receive before the GP earns carry. Standard is 8%. Below 8% net IRR, the GP gets zero carry.
Lock-up / J-Curve
Private funds have lock-up periods (capital is illiquid). The J-curve describes early negative returns (as fees are charged and capital deployed) that later become positive as exits occur.
Vintage Year
The year a fund starts deploying capital. Important context for evaluating returns — a 2009 fund had a great entry point; a 2007 fund did not.

Credit / Fixed Income

SOFR
Secured Overnight Financing Rate. The benchmark floating rate that replaced LIBOR. Private credit loans are typically priced as SOFR + spread (e.g., SOFR + 400bps = "S+400").
Senior Secured / First Lien
The highest-priority debt. If a company defaults, first lien holders are paid back before everyone else. Lowest yield but best protection.
Subordinated / Mezzanine
Debt that ranks below senior secured. Gets paid after first lien in a default. Higher yield to compensate for additional risk.
BDC (Business Development Company)
Publicly traded vehicle that invests in private credit. Provides retail investors some access to direct lending. Examples: Ares Capital (ARCC), Blue Owl Capital (OBDC).

Wealth Management

AUM
Assets under management. Total value of client assets the firm manages. CCA's revenue scales directly with AUM growth.
Fiduciary
Legal obligation to act in the client's best interest. RIAs like CCA are fiduciaries. Wirehouses historically were not (suitability standard only).
Qualified Purchaser (QP)
An individual with $5M+ in investments or an institution with $25M+. QPs can invest in a wider range of private funds than accredited investors alone.
Estate Planning
Legal and financial structuring to transfer wealth to heirs with minimal tax impact. Includes trusts (revocable, irrevocable, GRATs), gifting strategies, and charitable vehicles.
DAF (Donor-Advised Fund)
A charitable giving account. Client donates assets, gets an immediate tax deduction, and grants to charities over time. Popular among UHNW clients for philanthropic strategy.
Family Office
A private wealth management firm serving one (SFO) or multiple (MFO) ultra-wealthy families. CCA provides family office-equivalent services as an RIA.
