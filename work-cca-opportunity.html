Executive Summary

The buildout of AI computing infrastructure represents one of the most capital-intensive investment cycles in modern history. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have collectively committed over $300 billion in AI-related capex for 2025 alone — and third-party data center operators are struggling to keep pace with demand. This imbalance between supply and demand has created a compelling entry point for private credit: senior secured loans to data center developers and operators at historically wide spreads, backed by long-term contracts with investment-grade tenants.

For UHNW portfolios, this opportunity offers something increasingly rare: attractive risk-adjusted returns that are structurally uncorrelated to public markets, with predictable cash flows from creditworthy counterparties. The case for allocation is strong. The question is which managers and structures are worth the illiquidity premium — and that is precisely what this analysis addresses.

Market Context

The AI Infrastructure Build-Out

The shift to AI-native computing requires fundamentally different infrastructure than traditional cloud workloads. Training large language models demands dense GPU compute clusters with enormous power loads, specialized cooling, and low-latency networking. Existing hyperscaler data centers were not designed for this. The result is a multi-year construction cycle that is overwhelmingly funded by private capital.

The numbers are staggering in scale: Microsoft alone committed $80 billion to AI data centers in 2025. Google announced $75 billion. Meta guided to $60–65 billion. And these figures represent only the hyperscalers — a parallel buildout is underway among independent colocation operators (Equinix, Digital Realty), dedicated AI infrastructure companies (CoreWeave, Lambda Labs), and new entrants backed by sovereign wealth funds.

The Private Credit Gap

Traditional financing channels — bank loans and investment-grade bonds — cannot move fast enough or flex enough to meet the idiosyncratic needs of these deals. Construction timelines are compressed. Power interconnection requirements vary by site. Tenant credit profiles differ dramatically. Private credit, with its ability to move quickly and structure bespoke solutions, has stepped into this gap at scale. Direct lenders are writing senior secured loans against long-term leases (often 10–15 years) from investment-grade hyperscalers at spreads of 300–500 basis points over SOFR — significantly wider than comparable syndicated credit.

Investment Thesis

01
Structural demand with constrained supply
AI compute demand is doubling roughly every 18 months. New data center capacity takes 3–5 years to bring online at scale due to power permitting, equipment lead times, and construction. This supply/demand imbalance is structural, not cyclical, and supports borrower leverage to pay premium rates for private capital that can move quickly.
02
Senior secured loans backed by investment-grade leases
The typical deal structure involves a first-lien loan secured by a data center asset whose revenue is underpinned by a long-term lease from Microsoft, Google, or Amazon. These tenants carry investment-grade ratings (AAA/AA). The credit risk on the underlying cash flows is effectively sovereign-adjacent. Private credit earns the spread for complexity and illiquidity, not for taking credit risk on the tenant.
03
Low correlation to public market volatility
Private credit to data center operators has virtually no beta to equity markets. Returns are driven by contractual cash flows, not public sentiment. For UHNW portfolios that are already heavily exposed to public equities (often 50–70% of liquid wealth), this is a meaningful diversifier. The Sharpe ratio profile of senior secured private credit historically exceeds that of high-yield bonds at comparable nominal returns.
04
Manager selection matters more than the asset class bet
Not all private credit managers have AI infrastructure origination networks or the technical underwriting capability to evaluate power agreements, cooling specs, and tenant credit. Selecting a manager with proprietary deal flow — through hyperscaler relationships, engineering expertise, or developer networks — is the key alpha driver. This is where rigorous due diligence separates the top-quartile from the median.
“The question isn’t whether AI infrastructure needs capital — it clearly does. The question is whether the structure of the deal adequately compensates for the complexity and illiquidity.”

Risk Assessment

Risk Factor Severity Mitigant
Hyperscaler demand reversal Medium Long-term lease commitments (10–15yr) pre-date loan origination; tenant credit is investment-grade regardless of AI spending cycles. Demand reversal hurts equity, not senior debt.
Power and permitting risk High Evaluate lenders who underwrite only post-interconnection-agreement assets. Construction-stage loans (pre-power) carry significantly higher risk and require higher spreads to compensate.
Technology obsolescence Medium Senior secured lenders have short to mid-length holds (3–5yr). Facility upgrades are tenant obligation in most leases. Debt is typically repaid before technology risk materializes.
Illiquidity Medium For UHNW clients with $25M+ in assets, a 10–15% alternatives allocation in illiquid private credit is appropriate and well within liquidity management norms.
Rate sensitivity Low Most private credit loans are floating-rate (SOFR + spread), meaning the income stream increases as rates rise. Rising rate environments are broadly positive for direct lenders.
Manager concentration / key-person Medium Diversify across 2–3 managers with complementary origination strategies. Review key-person provisions in fund documents before committing capital.

Portfolio Fit for UHNW Clients

A well-constructed UHNW portfolio ($50M+) typically allocates 20–35% to alternatives. Within that bucket, private credit has grown rapidly because it delivers bond-like income with equity-adjacent returns, while maintaining low correlation to public markets. AI infrastructure private credit sits at the attractive end of the risk-return spectrum within this category: it is not venture capital, not distressed debt, and not illiquid real estate. It is structured lending to essential infrastructure backed by the largest, most creditworthy companies in the world.

For clients in wealth preservation mode, the senior secured structure protects principal. For clients in growth mode, the 10–13% net return profile competes favorably with mid-market private equity while requiring a shorter hold period and less equity risk. The asset class is also well-suited to clients with concentrated equity positions who want to reduce correlated risk without sacrificing return.

Recommendation

AI infrastructure private credit warrants a place in UHNW portfolios as a core alternatives allocation. The structural demand drivers are real, the deal structures are sound at the senior secured level, and the return profile — 10–13% net — is compelling relative to the risk being underwritten. The asset class is not without risks: power permitting, construction exposure, and manager selection are the material concerns.

Based on this evaluation, Blue Owl Technology Finance and HPS Investment Partners are the two managers most worth a deeper diligence conversation. Both have built proprietary AI infrastructure origination channels, both have demonstrated discipline on deal selection, and both offer minimum commitment thresholds appropriate for the $10–25M alternatives sleeve of a UHNW portfolio.

Key Takeaway

The best private credit opportunities in AI infrastructure aren’t about betting on AI. They’re about lending senior secured capital against long-term leases from Microsoft, Google, and Amazon — and getting paid 10–13% to do it. The AI story is the demand driver; the investment structure is the protection. For UHNW clients, the illiquidity premium is real, the diversification benefit is meaningful, and the risk is manageable. The key variable is manager selection, which is precisely the work a boutique RIA earns its fee on.
