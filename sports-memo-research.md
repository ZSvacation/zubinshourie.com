### Typical Deal Structures in Sports Private Credit

**Loan-to-Value (LTV) & Leverage:**
- **Comparable to 2nd-lien debt:** Higher interest rates, largely unsecured or limited collateral
- **League debt limits create headroom:** NFL allows max $1.2B debt for controlling stake; NBA allows $475M; constraints provide borrowing capacity

**Collateral Types:**
- Future revenue streams (ticket sales, sponsorships, seat deposits)
- Player transfer receivables (European models; $5B+ annual transfer market)
- Minority stake equity positions
- Cross-collateralization between real estate and media income
- Franchise media rights (often restricted due to league rules)

**Interest Rates:**
- Range not precisely quantified in current research but positioned as higher-yielding than traditional corporate credit (commensurate with 2nd-lien/unsecured positioning)

**Tenor:**
- Typically 5–7 year facilities with extensions
- Aligned with franchise holding periods and revenue ramp expectations

**Covenants:**
- Attendance thresholds
- Sponsorship renewal metrics
- Season-ticket deposit requirements
- Revenue performance baskets
- Often "covenant-lite" structures to accommodate ownership and operational flexibility

**Key Limitation:** League leverage restrictions mean franchise assets cannot directly serve as collateral; lenders must structure around team performance metrics and cash flows rather than asset seizure rights

**UHNW Preferred Access:** Direct co-investments (often $2M–$10M+) alongside fund vehicles

2. **Sixth Street Sports & Entertainment:** Equity-focused (though some debt structures); continuing originations

3. **Blue Owl GP Strategic Capital:** Minority stakes in professional sports; broader capital vehicles

4. **Emerging Specialist Funds:** Numerous mid-market and boutique credit managers launching sports-focused funds; names and terms often remain confidential pre-launch

---

### Liquidity Terms

**Typical Structure:**
- **Lock-up:** 3–7 years
- **Secondary liquidity:** Fund-level secondary market for LP positions; limited depth
- **Distributions:** Quarterly or semi-annual from debt interest; equity upside distributions at exit
- **Exit horizon:** 5–10 years on average

**Franchise-Level Liquidity Challenges:**
- Limited number of franchise sales annually (5–15 per major league)
- Narrow buyer pool relative to private equity market
- Minority stakes in major-league franchises more tradable than smaller-league or emerging-league stakes
- NFL has 6-year minimum holding period before sales; other leagues lack such restrictions

## 5. KEY RISKS (Specific)

### League Leverage Restrictions

**NFL:** Maximum $1.2 billion debt allowed for any controlling stake purchaser
- Recently raised from $500M to current level
- Constrains acquisitions financed primarily through debt
- Creates ceiling on LTV; limits refinancing optionality

**NBA:** $475 million debt limit per club
- Raised by $150M in recent cycle
- Implied debt/EBITDA of ~8x at midpoint EBITDA (~$60M/team)
- Prohibits pledge of team assets as acquisition financing collateral

**MLB & NHL:** Less detailed public guidance; similar restrictions in place

**Impact on Credit:** Lenders cannot rely on team assets for recovery; must structure against cash flows and revenue metrics

---

### Valuation Concentration Risk (Media Rights Dependency)

**Revenue Concentration:**
- NFL: 70% of revenue from domestic media deals
- NBA: 54% of revenue from media deals
- MLB: 46% of revenue from media deals
- NHL: 31% of revenue from media deals (more balanced regional/local)

**Valuation Multiples Risk:**
- NBA team valuation multiples jumped from 4.2x revenue (2013) to 12.9x (2025) — 3x increase
- Much of the increase is driven by confidence in media rights growth trajectory
- Any disruption to media rights deal assumptions could compress valuations significantly

**Macro Drivers:**
- Technology platform competition (Amazon, Apple, Netflix, etc.) has driven aggressive bidding
- Consolidation in media industry could reduce competitive bidding pool
- Consumer cord-cutting and streaming competition could pressure future rights values
- Regulatory uncertainty (FCC actions; antitrust scrutiny on mega-deals)

---

### Media Rights Renewal Risk

**NFL Specific:**
- Current deal runs through 2033, but opt-outs possible after 2028–2029
- Commissioner Goodell indicated potential early renegotiation starting 2026
- Risk of early renegotiation due to shifting competitive landscape
- Non-NFL sports properties face significant headwind as NFL prepares to consume large share of media budgets in early renegotiation cycle

**NBA Specific:**
- Locked into 11-year deal through 2035–36; very low near-term renewal risk
- Amazon's entry as primary streaming partner represents structural shift

**MLB Specific:**
- Only three-year deal (2026–2028); significant renewal risk in 2028
- Regional sports network (RSN) weakness creates local media revenue headwind
- Smaller overall media footprint vs. NFL/NBA

**Broader Risk:** Non-renewal or significant step-down in rights values would compress franchise cash flows and valuations; downstream impact on debt service capacity for leverage lenders

---

### Liquidity / Exit Risk

**Structural Challenges:**
- Limited franchise sale volume: 5–15 transactions per major league per year
- Valuation uncertainty in minority stake secondary markets
- No standardized trading venues for sports minority stakes

**League-Specific:**
- NFL: 6-year minimum hold period before stake sales; creates friction for interim exits
- NBA/MLB/MLS: No explicit holding period restrictions, but limited buyer pool

**Market Depth Risk:**
- Major-league franchises (top-tier NFL, NBA, MLB teams) have deeper buyer pools
- Secondary franchises and non-major-league stakes (MLS, NWSL, emerging sports) face significantly higher exit risk
- Institutional investor base continues to grow, but still nascent relative to traditional buyout/growth equity

**Financing Risk for Lenders:** Minority stake exits may take 7–10+ years; debt structures must accommodate extended holds or include interim liquidity facilities

---

### Operator / Performance Risk

**Team Management & Results:**
- On-field performance drives fan engagement, attendance, merchandise, and sponsorship renewables
- Poor competitive performance can suppress season-ticket renewals and sponsorship uptake
- Management quality and coaching decisions material to cash flow stability

**Labor Cost Escalation:**
- Player salary inflation tracks media rights growth and team revenue growth
- Salary caps/floors in NFL/NBA limit cost control flexibility
- MLB has no salary cap; unbounded labor cost exposure

**Sponsorship Renewal Risk:**
- Corporate sponsor renewals tied to team performance, market conditions, and marketing effectiveness
- Multi-year sponsorship concentration: loss of major sponsor can significantly impact revenues

## Summary: Investment Thesis Considerations

### Tailwinds
1. **Decade of media rights growth underway:** NFL (2023–2033), NBA (2025–2036), MLB (2026–2028, renewable) all locked in with multi-billion dollar valuations
2. **Institutional capital influx:** KKR, Sixth Street, Ares, Blue Owl, and emerging funds deploying significant capital; creditworthiness of sponsors improving
3. **Valuation appreciation:** Franchises appreciating 8–18% YoY; leverage capacity follows; refinancing opportunities
4. **Yield profile:** Higher-yielding debt instruments in growing asset class with limited supply
5. **Inflation hedge:** Sports assets display real asset characteristics; revenues tied to sponsorship and media prices that grow with inflation

### Headwinds
1. **Media rights concentration risk:** 50–70% of revenue from media deals; consumer cord-cutting and streaming competition create downside tail risk
2. **League leverage restrictions:** NFL, NBA, MLB limit debt capacity per franchise; constrains overall market size and leverage potential
3. **Liquidity constraints:** Limited franchise transaction volume; exits often require 5–10+ year holds; secondary market for stakes immature
4. **Regulatory uncertainty:** FCC scrutiny on mega-deals; antitrust concerns on consolidation; potential policy shifts
5. **Macro sensitivity:** Sports franchises correlate with GDP, discretionary spending, corporate sponsorship demand; recession scenario could compress valuations and cash flows
