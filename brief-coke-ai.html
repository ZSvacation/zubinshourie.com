Coca-Cola's AI Christmas Ad Was a Perfect Case Study in What Not to Do

The Daily Brief · March 11, 2026

Last year, Coca-Cola ran a holiday campaign using AI-generated video to remake its iconic "Holidays Are Coming" truck spot — and the internet responded with one word: soulless. The backlash wasn't just aesthetic. People weren't upset because it was AI. They were upset because it was obviously AI, and Coke used it on the one brand touchpoint that requires the most care: the moment of the year when the brand is supposed to feel warm, human, and timeless. That's the worst possible place to cut corners on craft.

The Specific Problem With Holiday Advertising

The original Coca-Cola "Holidays Are Coming" spot first aired in 1995. It's since run in dozens of countries over 30 consecutive years. That kind of accumulated cultural weight isn't just nostalgic — it's load-bearing. The truck, the lights, the music, and the warmth aren't just aesthetic choices; they're the delivery mechanism for an emotional experience that hundreds of millions of people have tied to a specific time of year. When you disrupt that with something that looks and feels wrong at a pixel level, you don't just miss the moment. You actively erode the brand equity it took three decades to build.

"You cannot outsource brand truth to a diffusion model and expect it to land."

The strategic lesson is clear once you see it, but a lot of brand teams are still missing it. AI is an execution tool, not a brand tool. You can use AI to generate product descriptions, optimize ad targeting, scale localized creative, or generate hundreds of CTA variants. That's what it's built for. Where it consistently fails is in the moments that require what brand strategists call "brand truth" — the emotional territory a brand has spent decades earning the right to occupy. For Coca-Cola, that territory is warmth, togetherness, and nostalgia. You cannot outsource that to a diffusion model and expect it to land. The pixels are wrong, the lighting is uncanny, and audiences feel it even when they can't articulate why.

The Slop Problem

The real danger isn't that AI will produce obviously bad advertising. It's that it'll produce efficient, technically competent, invisible advertising — and invisible is death for a brand at Coke's scale. Merriam-Webster's 2025 word of the year was "slop," a perfect descriptor for the flood of AI-generated content that looks and sounds interchangeable. In a world of slop, distinctiveness becomes the rarest thing a brand can hold.

The brands that win this decade won't be the ones that adopted AI earliest. They'll be the ones that were most disciplined about where they chose not to use it — and that instinct is what separates a brand strategist from a production manager.

What Good AI Integration Actually Looks Like

The contrast case worth studying is how some brands are using AI in the pre-production phase rather than in final output. AI-generated storyboards and reference images to communicate a creative direction to a production team. AI to rapid-prototype fifteen variations of a tagline before a copywriter refines the three that actually resonate. AI to dynamically personalize a banner ad's background image based on geography — not the emotionally resonant foreground subject, just the contextual backdrop. That's the distinction. AI as a tool for speed and iteration in places where craft doesn't carry the emotional weight; human judgment and craft in the places that do.

For Coke, the failure wasn't technological. The technology worked as intended — it produced a technically functional video. The failure was strategic. Someone in the brand approval chain decided that efficiency was more important than the specific kind of quality this particular touchpoint requires. That's a judgment error, not a technology error. And it's the same error that's going to play out across dozens of major brands over the next two years as the cost of AI-generated content drops and the pressure to use it increases.
