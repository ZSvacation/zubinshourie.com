Oil at $90 and Climbing — The Energy Shock That Changes the Fed's Math

Bloomberg · CNBC · PBS NewsHour · Al Jazeera Economy

When the US and Israel launched strikes on Iran on February 28, the oil market had been trading around $70/barrel. Within 72 hours, Brent crude crossed $90. By March 5, Bloomberg was reporting prices above $100 for the first time since Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022. The mechanism is straightforward and brutal: the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of global oil and LNG passes daily, is now a war zone — and the tanker operators, insurers, and shipping firms running that traffic have largely decided the risk isn't worth it.

The economic math that follows isn't complicated. The IMF has spelled it out directly: every 10% increase in sustained oil prices adds 0.4 percentage points to global inflation and shaves 0.2% off worldwide GDP. If Brent holds at current levels, the world is looking at nearly a full percentage point of additional inflation pressure layered on top of an economy that was only just cooling down. For central banks that were preparing to cut rates in the first half of 2026, this is a serious problem — the conditions that justified easing are evaporating fast.

"Analysts estimate traders are currently paying a $14/barrel war premium — and that premium doesn't go away until the strait reopens."

What It Means at the Pump — and Beyond

US retail gas prices crossed $3.32/gallon nationally by March 7. Analysts at GasBuddy and AAA are projecting $3.50+ within weeks if the conflict continues at its current intensity. That's a roughly 15-20 cent increase at the pump in under two weeks — a number consumers feel immediately and that shows up quickly in consumer confidence surveys.

But the real downstream effects are broader. Fuel is an input cost for essentially every physical product — food, manufacturing, logistics, construction. A sustained $100+ oil environment would push producer prices higher across the board, filter through to consumer prices over the following quarters, and give the Fed political cover to hold rates higher for longer. The Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan desks were already revising their 2026 rate-cut forecasts when this data hit.

Safe-Haven Rotation and What to Watch

Gold and silver caught meaningful bids during the week as institutional money rotated into safety trades. Energy sector equities — refiners, domestic producers, nuclear plays — saw significant inflows. Names exposed to uranium and alternative energy stand to benefit in any scenario where Middle Eastern supply remains constrained for an extended period. The S&P dropped 1.33% and the Nasdaq fell 1.59% on the week, but the story underneath the index moves is a sector rotation, not a panic: money is moving out of rate-sensitive growth and into hard assets and energy infrastructure.
