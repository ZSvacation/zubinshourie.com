50% Tariff Threat

Trump Threatens 50% Tariffs on Iran Weapons Suppliers — Ceasefire Frays as Israel Kills 254 in Lebanon

The two-week US-Iran ceasefire is already showing its fractures. In a Wednesday announcement that rattled markets before they could finish celebrating, President Trump declared a 50% tariff on "any and all" goods from countries supplying military weapons to Iran — no exclusions, no exemptions, effective immediately. The primary targets are Russia and China. Analysts are questioning the legal authority behind the threat, but the market response was unambiguous: equity futures sold off, and the VIX jumped back above 23.

The geopolitical situation is even more precarious. Israel launched its largest offensive yet against Lebanon overnight — killing at least 254 people, striking Beirut directly, and eliminating the nephew of Hezbollah leader Naim Qassem. Both Trump and Netanyahu have been explicit: Lebanon is not included in the Iran ceasefire. Gulf states reported new drone activity despite the declared truce, and the Strait of Hormuz remains partially constrained. Oil is erasing nearly all of Wednesday's 16% plunge. The 24-hour relief trade has a very short shelf life.

Portfolio read: The tariff escalation adds a new risk variable on top of the existing energy shock. PLTR, defense-adjacent names, and hard assets continue to look well-positioned; high-multiple tech that surged yesterday on the ceasefire is unwinding early. Watch crude's close today — if WTI approaches $100 again, risk assets face real pressure heading into the weekend.

Disney Resets

Disney Is Cutting 1,000 Jobs Under New CEO D'Amaro — What It Signals About Where Hollywood Is Heading

Disney announced it's eliminating up to 1,000 positions in the coming months, with marketing taking the heaviest hit. The cuts come less than a month after Josh D'Amaro officially took over as CEO from Bob Iger on March 18th — making this his first major restructuring move. D'Amaro is a parks-and-experiences man, not a content guy, and the early signals suggest he's going to run Disney more like a hospitality conglomerate than a traditional Hollywood studio.

The context here matters: this isn't a panic move — it's part of a broader industry-wide reset. Sony Pictures confirmed it's also cutting hundreds of positions. Every major studio is dealing with the same structural pressure: streaming eroded the old theatrical-to-cable-to-home-video revenue waterfall, content costs are still too high relative to subscriber conversion, and the macro environment under active geopolitical stress is complicating the math for large-cap media. Iger's final act was stabilizing the balance sheet; D'Amaro's first act is right-sizing the org chart.

For you specifically, Zubin: cuts in Disney's marketing department are a real signal about how the industry sees the function going forward. Studios are automating performance marketing (paid social, programmatic) and relying increasingly on platforms — Netflix, Amazon — for discovery rather than traditional broadcast marketing. The value is migrating toward brand marketing, talent relations, and experiential — which is exactly where parks-focused D'Amaro's instincts lie. The conversational AI ad layer that OpenAI and Google are building this week is the same structural shift from a different angle: the middlemen in content discovery are being disintermediated. Learn the strategic layer, not just the execution layer.

Watch whether the layoffs extend to content teams or stay contained to corporate and marketing. If content gets cut, Disney is pivoting to a "fewer, bigger bets" model. If it stays in overhead, this is just normal CEO efficiency signaling. The next 6 months of greenlight decisions will be the real tell.
