Iran Pause

Trump Delays Iran Strike Deadline, Futures Rally — But the War Risk Premium Isn't Gone

President Trump extended the deadline for US strikes on Iran's energy infrastructure, triggering a risk-on move in overnight futures — S&P up 0.55%, Nasdaq +0.6%, Dow +0.5%. The decision bought time for diplomatic back-channels, but the Pentagon is separately weighing additional troop deployments to the region, keeping markets on edge.

The broader economic damage from the US-Iran conflict is already real. Brent crude is near $108/barrel, Fitch estimates a prolonged conflict shaves 0.8% off global GDP within a year, and the OECD cut its 2026 global growth forecast from 3.3% to 2.9%. G20 inflation is now projected at 4.0% — 1.2 points above prior expectations — largely driven by energy.

For markets, today's question isn't whether the conflict is over — it isn't — it's whether the pause holds long enough for earnings season to reclaim the narrative. Watch oil. WTI slipped to ~$94 overnight, which is meaningful. If Brent breaks below $100 on sustained de-escalation, equities have room to run.

Economics · March 27 · China

It's Official: China Surpasses the US as World's Largest Economy by PPP

The IMF confirmed China has overtaken the United States in purchasing power parity terms, marking a seismic shift in the global economic order. While PPP comparisons are imperfect — they smooth over currency differences — the milestone carries enormous symbolic weight at a moment when US-China competition is heating up across trade, tech, and military posture. China's central bank followed the news by pledging deeper reforms and capital support to manage financial stability risks.

Today’s Rabbit Hole
Sports Business · AI · Fan Engagement
AI Owns Sports

AI Isn't Coming for Sports — It's Already Running It: The $4B Transformation Nobody's Fully Clocked Yet

The AI-in-sports market hit roughly $4 billion in 2026 and is on a trajectory to $11 billion by 2030 — and the numbers undersell what's actually happening. Over 80% of professional sports organizations have deployed AI in some operational capacity. We're past the 'pilot program' era. AI is now core infrastructure for everything from ticket pricing (dynamically adjusted in real time based on weather, opponent, and secondary market data) to injury prevention systems that predict risk with 85-90% accuracy using wearable biometrics. The Detroit Pistons running the East isn't just basketball — it's what happens when front offices get serious about data.

The more interesting action is in broadcasting and fan engagement. AI is automating multi-feed production at scale — smaller broadcast crews, more personalized viewing angles, real-time highlight generation that used to require an entire editing team. And on the fan side, the shift from 'website' to 'owned app' as the primary digital touchpoint is underway, driven by AI personalization engines that learn what you watch, when you engage, and what you'll pay for. The leagues that crack this — and the Cowboys and Lakers are already deep in it — will extract dramatically more revenue per fan than they do today.

For someone going into sports marketing, this is the most important structural shift in the industry. The job of a sports marketer is increasingly a data job. Campaigns are built around audience segmentation that would've been impossible five years ago. Sponsorship activation is measurable in ways it never was. Athletes are brands with AI-assisted content pipelines. The entry-level roles that existed in 2022 are being compressed, but the ceiling for people who understand both the creative and the data side is higher than it's ever been.

What to watch: The NCAA's lawsuit against DraftKings over 'March Madness' trademark use is a microcosm of a bigger fight — who owns the language of sports fandom at the intersection of betting, media, and AI-generated content? The leagues want full control of their IP in a world where AI can generate infinite amounts of branded content. That legal and commercial battle will define the next decade of sports business.
