Hormuz Crunch

Strait of Hormuz Closure Sends Brent Crude to $109; Oil Markets Brace for $150 Scenario

The US-Israel conflict with Iran has delivered its most direct economic blow yet: the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint through which roughly 20% of the world's crude passes — is effectively closed. Brent crude surged to $109.03, WTI to $111.54, with Goldman and JPMorgan both running $150 scenario models. This isn't hypothetical anymore.

The IMF has already downgraded the global growth outlook, pushing inflation forecasts higher across developed markets. The ECB is now weighing whether it needs to *tighten* again — a few months ago, nobody was saying that.

For your portfolio: GLD and NLR are both beneficiaries of this environment. Energy-heavy ETFs look interesting. XLU tends to hold up when stagflation fears spike. Watch the Fed's next communication carefully — their easing bias just got a lot harder to defend.

Geopolitics · Energy · Markets
The Hormuz Bet

The Strait of Hormuz Is the Most Important 21-Mile Stretch of Water in the World — Here's What Closing It Actually Means

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point, sits between Iran and Oman, and is responsible for moving roughly 20% of the world's crude oil and about 17% of liquefied natural gas. When markets started pricing in its closure this week, Brent crude jumped past $109 in a single session. The math isn't complicated: remove a fifth of global oil supply from the market and every economy on earth immediately feels it — not in months, in days.

This has happened before, sort of. Iran has threatened to close the strait repeatedly since the 1980s, and the US Fifth Fleet has been stationed in Bahrain for decades specifically to prevent it. But the difference now is that the conflict has escalated beyond the usual threshold, and the US is a direct participant rather than a mediator. That changes the calculus entirely. In past episodes, the threat alone was enough to move oil $10-15. An actual, prolonged closure would be the largest energy supply shock since the 1973 OPEC embargo — potentially larger, because global consumption is far higher now.

For someone studying business and watching markets, this is the case study that connects geopolitics to finance in the most direct way possible. When the strait is threatened, watch: oil majors (ExxonMobil, Chevron) spike; airlines, shipping companies, and manufacturers — anyone who consumes energy — get crushed; gold and defense stocks rally; the Fed's job gets exponentially harder. The energy-inflation-rate loop is the core macro story of 2026, and Hormuz is its geographic center. Your NLR and GLD holdings are both sitting in the right neighborhood for this environment.

What to watch: France and South Korea's joint diplomatic push is the most credible off-ramp right now. If they get Iran and the US back to a table, oil reverses hard and risk assets exhale. If the conflict widens — particularly if more regional players get involved — $150 oil becomes consensus, the Fed is frozen, and 2026 GDP forecasts get cut again. This is the hinge point for Q2.
