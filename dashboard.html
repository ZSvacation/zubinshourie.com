Top Stories

Fed Holds at 3.50–3.75%; Dot Plot Shows Seven Members Now See Zero Cuts This Year

The FOMC held rates unchanged for the second consecutive meeting, as expected — but the updated Summary of Economic Projections told a more hawkish story. The Fed raised its 2026 inflation forecast to 2.7% (from 2.4% in December) and pushed its 2% target out to 2028. Seven members now project no rate cuts this year; seven project one cut. The lone dissenter was Stephen Miran, who wanted a cut.

Powell cited “elevated uncertainty” from Middle East developments and said higher energy prices from the Iran war were a near-term inflation driver — though he characterized the effect as potentially “transitory.” Markets didn’t buy it. The S&P 500 fell 1.36%, Nasdaq dropped 1.46%, and WTI surged toward $100 on a container ship fire in the Strait of Hormuz. The 10-year yield climbed to 4.26%. Powell also noted he would serve as Chair pro-tem if incoming nominee Kevin Warsh isn’t confirmed by May.

Today’s Rabbit Hole

The Fed Called the Iran Oil Shock “Transitory.” Here’s Why That Word Has Baggage.

Powell used careful language today — he said the near-term rise in inflation from the Iran conflict may prove “transitory,” meaning the supply shock could resolve and prices could normalize. That’s a defensible position. It’s also the same framing the Fed used in 2021 when it called pandemic-era inflation temporary, then spent the next two years hiking rates to 5.25% to undo the damage of waiting too long.

The stakes of that word choice are real. If the Hormuz disruption is short-lived — a ceasefire, a diplomatic deal, the strait reopens — then oil retreats, CPI moderates, and the Fed gets a cut in the second half. If the conflict grinds on (Day 19 today), energy prices stay elevated, they compound into freight costs, into food, into services inflation. The dot plot already shows seven members who think zero cuts are appropriate this year. One more bad PPI print and that number goes up.

The second variable is the labor market. February’s payroll print was -92,000 — the first negative print in years — partly distorted by a 31,000-nurse strike in California, but the unemployment rate still ticked up to 4.4%. The Fed has two mandates. Right now both are flashing yellow simultaneously: prices too high, jobs softening. That’s a genuinely uncomfortable place to be when you’re the central bank and there’s a war. The Powell era ends in May. Whoever Kevin Warsh is going to be inherits this.
