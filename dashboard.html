Geopolitics · April 6 · US-Iran Escalation
Jets Down

Iran Shoots Down Two US Military Aircraft — First Combat Loss in Over Two Decades — as Emergency Ceasefire Talks Begin

The conflict crossed a major threshold over the weekend. Iran shot down two American military jets, marking the first time US warplanes have been downed in combat since the 2003 Iraq invasion. The White House responded with a profanity-laden post from Trump on Truth Social threatening strikes on Iranian power plants and bridges. US forces carried out a high-risk rescue mission for the downed pilots overnight.

Yet the most significant development may be what's happening behind the scenes. Multiple mediators — Pakistan, Turkey, and Egypt — are now presenting a formal ceasefire framework that would include an immediate halt to hostilities and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. Iran and the US have both reportedly received the proposal. Markets are treating this as the primary scenario: equities fell hard at the open but are clawing back, gold is fading, and BTC is up nearly 3% on the optimism.

The April 7 deadline Trump set last week remains technically in effect. Three outcomes remain possible: full ceasefire (oil crashes, equities surge), partial deal buying time (range-bound), or US military escalation on Iranian infrastructure (oil spikes toward $130, equities sell off hard). The tape is currently pricing the middle path.

Cooper Flagg Drops 51 Points to Become the First Teenager in NBA History to Reach 50 in a Game

Aircraft Down

Iran Just Shot Down Two US Jets. Here's Why That Changes the Entire Calculus — and Why Markets Are Still Betting on a Deal

The last time a US military aircraft was shot down in combat was during the 2003 invasion of Iraq. That fact alone signals how significantly the US-Iran conflict escalated over the weekend. Two American jets downed, a rescue mission in hostile airspace overnight, and a president threatening to destroy Iranian power infrastructure. By any historical standard, this is a full-blown hot war between the United States and Iran.

And yet the market is recovering off the lows. That seems paradoxical until you understand the ceasefire dynamics in play. The simultaneous development driving the tape this morning is that a formal three-party ceasefire proposal — constructed by Pakistani, Turkish, and Egyptian mediators — has been placed in front of both governments. The framework reportedly includes an immediate halt to hostilities and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for specific security guarantees. Both sides are said to be reviewing it. Markets are pricing roughly 60% odds on some version of a deal getting done before conditions deteriorate further.

The portfolio read: the BTC/gold divergence this morning is a clean risk-signal. BTC up 2.67%, gold down 1.03% — that's the market saying "deal risk is rising, safe-haven premium is compressing." If a ceasefire lands, GLD and XLU give back recent gains, energy names correct, and growth tech gets a lifeline from a potential Fed pivot re-opening. If no deal by tomorrow night and Trump executes on the power plant threat, the reverse trade is violent: oil $130+, equities sell off 3-5%, gold spikes, and the consumer gets crushed by a second energy shock on top of an already-softening labor market. The risk is asymmetric and the window is short. Stay positioned but stay alert.

One thing worth watching that isn't getting enough coverage: North Korea. South Korea's intelligence agency now believes Kim Jong Un has positioned his teenage daughter as his designated successor. In a week where American military assets are being shot down and global attention is fully consumed by the Middle East, Pyongyang has unusually quiet conditions in which to move. Keep that tab open.
