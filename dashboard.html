Conflict Week 5 · Energy · $200 Scenario
The $200 Number

Why Analysts Are Seriously Modeling $200 Oil — and What That Would Actually Mean for Everything

Two weeks ago, $200 oil was a tail risk that analysts mentioned in footnotes to be thorough. Today it's a base-case scenario on several Wall Street desks, and the math is not complicated. Hormuz moves roughly 20% of the world's crude — about 20 million barrels per day. Right now, traffic has fallen from 150 vessels a day to 10–20. The world is currently short an estimated 4.5–5 million barrels per day, about 5% of global consumption. That number gets worse every week the strait stays effectively closed.

The 1973 OPEC embargo removed about 5 million barrels per day from global supply at a time when total global consumption was around 56 million barrels. That shock sent oil up 300% in four months, triggered recession in most developed economies, and kept inflation elevated for a decade. Global consumption now is roughly 103 million barrels per day — almost double. The disruption mechanism is the same, the global economy's dependence is larger, and this time there's no OPEC switch to flip back on. The question isn't whether $200 is mathematically possible. It's whether the political resolution happens fast enough to prevent it.

For your portfolio and your understanding of macro: the transmission mechanism is direct and fast. Oil above $150 sends jet fuel and diesel to record highs within days — that's airlines, logistics, agriculture (fertilizer), and manufacturing simultaneously getting hit. Consumer inflation spikes. The Fed, which already can't cut, is now actively weighing whether it needs to tighten into a slowing economy. That's the stagflation trap. Gold and defense hold. Risk assets don't. The only real off-ramp right now is Oman's proposed Hormuz monitoring protocol — a diplomatic face-saving mechanism that lets Iran reopen the strait without appearing to capitulate. Watch that story closely over the next 10 days. It is the most important 21-mile stretch of water on the planet right now, and everything else this quarter follows from what happens there.
