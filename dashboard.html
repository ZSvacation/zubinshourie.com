Hormuz Shock

Strait of Hormuz Disruption Sends Oil Past $111 — Global Slowflation Risk Now Front and Center

Military conflict involving the US, Israel, and Iran has effectively shut down maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint responsible for roughly one-fifth of global oil consumption and a significant share of LNG exports. Brent crude has surged past $111 per barrel (some spot markets showing $141+), with WTI tracking close behind. This isn't a temporary blip: UNCTAD is already warning of cascading trade disruptions across commodity chains.

The macro read is increasingly grim. Analysts are dusting off 'stagflation' as a real scenario — not a buzzword. Weak growth plus sticky inflation above central bank targets is exactly the cocktail the global economy can't afford right now. The Fed, which was already cautious heading into 2026, has effectively been pushed back to one cut this year, possibly one next year. Countries from Pakistan to Romania are scrambling to offset fuel prices with excise tax cuts.

For the portfolio: NLR and GLD are worth watching closely today. Energy disruption that threatens nuclear-as-alternative narrative could lift NLR longer term. Gold typically benefits in stagflation environments. PLTR and APLD face headwinds if AI data center energy costs keep rising — energy is already their biggest scaling constraint.

AI Is Coming for Sports Media Jobs — And the Industry Is Quietly Letting It Happen

The sports media industry is undergoing a structural transformation that looks gradual until it doesn't. AI is moving from post-production assistance — auto-generating highlights, clipping social content — into live production itself. Video language models can now perform real-time frame extraction, instant analysis, and broadcast packaging in-game. The question isn't whether AI can do some of this work. It's already doing it. The question is how fast leagues, networks, and talent agencies adapt their business models.

Here's what makes this moment different from previous waves of media technology: the compression of the production layer. Historically, a broadcast required an enormous crew — producers, editors, graphics operators, replay coordinators. AI is eating that crew from the bottom up. The first casualties are already visible: editing, transcription, content moderation. What comes next is more nuanced work — highlights selection, social packaging, even on-screen analysis assistance. The NBA, NFL, and MLB are all quietly running AI-assisted production experiments with their streaming partners.

For someone entering sports marketing and media in 2026, this is double-edged. The creative and strategic layer — talent representation, brand partnerships, narrative framing, community building — is still very much human work, and arguably worth more as AI commoditizes the production layer below it. The lesson from every previous wave of media disruption (radio → TV → streaming) is that the people who understood the new distribution layer earliest built the biggest advantages. Right now, that layer is personalized AI-powered content delivery: hyper-targeted highlights, custom fan feeds, real-time engagement triggers at scale.

What to watch: how leagues price their rights when AI can generate derivative content instantly, whether unions (NFLPA, NBPA) push for AI-use disclosure and revenue sharing clauses, and how talent agencies adapt their IP strategy as AI-generated athletes and influencers start competing for eyeballs. The companies at the intersection of sports, media, and AI infrastructure — think data rights holders, streaming platforms, and agentic AI companies — are where the next decade of value gets created.
