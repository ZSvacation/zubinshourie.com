Havana · Blackout · Uprising
Cuba Goes Dark

Cuba’s Grid Collapsed. The Protests Are the Largest Since 2021. What’s Actually Happening on the Island.

On March 16, Cuba’s entire electrical grid failed, plunging roughly 11 million people into darkness. The blackout came on the 10th consecutive day of protests — the largest sustained unrest since July 2021. The trigger is straightforward: Trump cut off Cuba’s oil access as part of a broader pressure campaign, and the island’s already fragile infrastructure couldn’t absorb the shock. Food shortages, no power, no water in many areas.

The protests have escalated rapidly. In Morón, demonstrators torched a local Communist Party headquarters — footage showed gunfire in the area, and five people were arrested. Across Havana neighborhoods, the cacerolazo tradition has returned: residents banging pots and pans at night, erecting barricades, burning trash. The government is calling it vandalism. Observers are calling it the most serious challenge to the regime in decades.

The context matters for anyone watching US foreign policy. The Cuba squeeze is happening simultaneously with the Iran war, the Hormuz blockade, and rising oil prices globally. Trump is applying maximum economic pressure on multiple fronts at once. Whether that produces regime change, humanitarian crisis, or both depends on variables that are moving fast. CNN, Al Jazeera, and Military.com all have deep coverage.
