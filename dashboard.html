Fragile Ceasefire

US-Iran Ceasefire Wobbles as Hormuz Shipping Stays Restricted — Goldman Warns of $115 Oil by Year-End

The two-week ceasefire between the US and Iran, announced Wednesday, triggered one of the largest single-day oil price drops in nearly six years — but by Thursday morning, traders are walking it back. Brent crude and WTI futures are both moving higher again as concerns mount that the truce is fragile, maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains restricted, and neither side has made meaningful moves toward a permanent deal.

Goldman Sachs wasted no time flagging the risk, issuing a call for $115/barrel crude by end of year if the ceasefire collapses. The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20% of global oil supply — any sustained blockade translates directly to energy inflation, which in turn pressures the Fed's rate path and squeezes margins across industrials, airlines, and consumer goods. European markets are already wobbling, with Italy's PM Meloni calling for temporary suspension of EU budget deficit rules if conflict flares again.

For your portfolio: NLR (nuclear energy) and GLD are two holdings that benefit from an 'oil risk premium' environment. UUUU (uranium) also worth watching — energy diversification narratives accelerate when crude spikes.

Disney Resets

Disney Is Cutting 1,000 Jobs Under New CEO D'Amaro — What It Signals About Where Hollywood Is Heading

Disney announced it's eliminating up to 1,000 positions in the coming months, with marketing taking the heaviest hit. The cuts come less than a month after Josh D'Amaro officially took over as CEO from Bob Iger on March 18th — making this his first major restructuring move. D'Amaro is a parks-and-experiences man, not a content guy, and the early signals suggest he's going to run Disney more like a hospitality conglomerate than a traditional Hollywood studio.

The context here matters: this isn't a panic move — it's part of a broader industry-wide reset. Sony Pictures confirmed it's also cutting hundreds of positions. Every major studio is dealing with the same structural pressure: streaming eroded the old theatrical-to-cable-to-home-video revenue waterfall, content costs are still too high relative to subscriber conversion, and the Iran war uncertainty is complicating the macro environment for large-cap media. Iger's final act was stabilizing the balance sheet; D'Amaro's first act is right-sizing the org chart.

For you specifically, Zubin: if you're targeting entertainment marketing as a career path, cuts in that department at Disney are a real signal about how the industry sees the function. Studios are increasingly automating performance marketing (paid social, programmatic) and relying on platforms themselves (Netflix, Amazon) for discovery rather than traditional broadcast marketing. The value is migrating toward brand marketing, talent relations, and experiential — which is exactly where parks-focused D'Amaro's instincts lie. Learn those skills.

Watch whether the layoffs extend to content teams or stay contained to corporate/marketing. If content gets cut, that tells you Disney is pivoting to a 'fewer, bigger bets' model. If it stays in overhead, this is just normal CEO efficiency signaling. The next 6 months of greenlight decisions will be the real tell.
