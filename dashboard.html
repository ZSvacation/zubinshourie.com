Hormuz Closed

Middle East War Reshapes Global Energy: Strait of Hormuz Largely Shut, Brent Briefly Tops $119, IMF Raises Recession Risk

The Iran-Israel-US conflict is no longer just a geopolitical story — it’s a full energy shock. The Strait of Hormuz, which carries roughly 20% of the world’s oil and seaborne LNG, is largely closed. Qatar’s LNG facility was struck, slashing export capacity 17% with repairs expected to take years. Kuwaiti oil refineries and Saudi’s Samref were also hit. Brent crude briefly spiked to $119/bbl before pulling back to ~$107 as signals of diplomatic movement emerged. The IEA triggered its largest-ever emergency oil reserve release in response.

The economic fallout is spreading fast. The ECB cut its 2026 eurozone GDP forecast to 0.9% and raised inflation projections to 2.6%. S&P Global lowered growth forecasts globally. The IMF is flagging rising recession risks for energy-import-dependent economies. JPMorgan warned that sustained $110/bbl oil would materially hit S&P 500 earnings. The US approved $16.46B in military sales to the UAE and Kuwait, both nations directly in the conflict zone.

Today’s Rabbit Hole
Hormuz · Energy · Global Economy
The Chokepoint

Twenty Percent of the World’s Oil Flows Through the Strait of Hormuz. Here’s What Happens When It Closes.

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. Through it flows roughly 17–21 million barrels of oil per day — about 20% of global supply — plus massive volumes of LNG from Qatar, which supplies Europe, Asia, and parts of the Americas. It is, in the bluntest possible terms, the most important stretch of water on the planet.

When it closes — even partially, even temporarily — the knock-on effects are swift and compounding. Oil prices spike, which raises transportation costs for everything, which raises inflation everywhere, which forces central banks to hold rates higher, which pressures equity valuations, which slows business investment. That chain reaction is playing out right now. Brent hit $119 this week. The IEA triggered its largest-ever emergency stock release. The ECB already cut its eurozone growth forecast for 2026 to 0.9%. The IMF is flagging debt distress risks for oil-import-dependent countries.

The deeper question is duration. A brief closure — weeks — is painful but survivable. A months-long shutdown reshapes global supply chains in ways that take years to unwind. Energy-intensive industries face margin compression. Countries without strategic reserves face genuine crises. The US, which has become a net energy exporter, is relatively insulated — but “relatively” is doing a lot of work in that sentence when your portfolio holds energy-adjacent names like NLR, XLU, UUUU, and GLD.
