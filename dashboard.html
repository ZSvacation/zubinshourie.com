Bridge Down

Iran's Tallest Bridge Collapses After Reported US Airstrikes — Tehran Threatens Retaliation Against American Allies

The US-Iran war escalated sharply overnight: Iran's tallest bridge — a landmark structure and symbol of the country's engineering ambitions — collapsed following reported American airstrikes. Iranian state television immediately broadcast warnings that US-allied nations across the Middle East could face direct retaliation. The collapse is being framed domestically in Iran as an attack on civilian infrastructure, handing hardliners significant political leverage to escalate.

This marks a meaningful shift from the Strait of Hormuz disruption that defined earlier this week. Moving from strategic chokepoints to landmark civilian infrastructure signals either a deliberate US messaging strategy — degrading Iran's symbolic as well as military assets — or an indication that collateral impact on the Iranian economy is no longer a constraint. Either way, the regional calculus changes immediately. Allies including Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Jordan are now calculating their exposure. Oil at $111 was the economic shock; this is the psychological one.

For investors: geopolitical risk premium stays elevated going into the long weekend with no market safety valve until Monday. Energy names, GLD, and defense sector remain in focus. The jobs report that drops this morning — with no market able to react — will add another variable to Monday's open. Brace for a volatile start to the week.

Today’s Rabbit Hole
Macro · Policy · Portfolio Strategy
The Stagflation Word

Stagflation Is Back in the Vocabulary — What It Actually Means and What It Does to a Portfolio

The word stagflation is back. It's been floating in financial media for weeks as oil prices pushed past $111, and now — with Iran's tallest bridge down and the conflict showing no sign of resolution — it's moving from commentary filler to genuine scenario-planning term. It's worth understanding what the word actually means and why it's difficult to navigate, because the policy toolkit that normally works in recessions or inflation spikes doesn't work cleanly here.

Stagflation is the combination of stagnant economic growth and persistent inflation — a scenario where the usual Fed playbook breaks. Normally, if inflation is high, you raise rates to cool demand. If growth is weak, you cut rates to stimulate. Stagflation forces you to choose: raise rates to fight inflation and risk choking off whatever growth remains, or cut rates to support the economy and let inflation run. The 1970s analog is instructive: oil price shocks driven by OPEC embargoes created the same structural bind, and it took a historically brutal rate-hiking cycle under Volcker — one that caused a deep recession — to break inflation's back.

What's different now: the inflation isn't coming from domestic demand overheating. It's supply-side, driven by an oil shock tied to a military conflict with no clear end date. That means rate hikes don't address the source of inflation — they just apply additional brake to an economy that may not need it. The Fed is effectively watching from the sidelines. One cut in 2026 is now the base case. The question is whether that cut ever arrives.

For a portfolio: historically, commodities (especially energy and gold), infrastructure, TIPS (inflation-protected treasuries), and value stocks in energy and materials outperform during stagflation. High-multiple growth names — particularly anything trading on future earnings several years out — underperform as discount rates stay elevated. Bitcoin's role is genuinely uncertain here: it trades like a risk asset in the short term but has a theoretical inflation-hedge narrative that some investors lean into over longer horizons. The honest answer is that it's an active area of debate. The cleaner plays are energy and real assets, which have direct fundamental tailwinds from the oil shock rather than narrative-based ones.
