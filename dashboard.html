Oil Shock

Iran's Strait of Hormuz Closure Sends Oil Past $108, Threatening Global Stagflation

WTI crude hit $108 a barrel this weekend — up 58% since the Israel-Iran conflict began and nearly double its price at the start of the year. The catalyst: Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint through which roughly one-fifth of global crude normally flows. Even with Iran reportedly allowing limited humanitarian passage, the market is treating full disruption as the base case.

The downstream effects are spreading fast. Airline and shipping costs are surging. Countries like Japan, India, and most of Europe — which depend heavily on Middle Eastern oil — are seeing import bills balloon and currencies weaken. Central banks that were hoping to ease are now watching inflation reaccelerate. The ECB and Fed are both leaning toward hikes. Spain, Germany, and Italy have already called on Brussels for a coordinated windfall tax on energy companies.

The macro read: this is textbook slowflation — weak growth, above-target inflation — with a real tail risk of full stagflation if the conflict drags through Q3. For portfolios: GLD is doing exactly what it should. NLR and XLU as energy/utility plays are worth watching. UUUU (uranium) likely benefits if nuclear gets re-priced as a stable alternative in the energy mix.

CFR · April 2026 · Trade Policy

One Year After Liberation Day: Bridge Tariffs Now 15%, Average Effective Rate at Highest Since 1972

The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs in February, but the administration quickly replaced them with Section 122 "bridge tariffs" of 10–15%, expected to run through July. The result: the average U.S. effective tariff rate is 5.6% — highest since 1972 — and households are absorbing an estimated $1,300 in additional costs this year. Companies with heavy international supply chains are in the crosshairs.

Markets · Energy · Geopolitics
The Hormuz Bet

The Strait of Hormuz Just Became the Most Dangerous Chokepoint in the Global Economy — Here's What That Actually Means

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point and handles roughly 20% of global crude oil shipments and 17% of liquefied natural gas. It is, structurally, the most critical single chokepoint in the energy world — more than Suez, more than Panama. When Iran closed it earlier this week, they didn't just threaten oil supply. They triggered a repricing event across every market that depends on stable energy costs, which is to say: all of them.

This isn't the first time Hormuz has been threatened. During the Tanker War of the 1980s, both the U.S. and Soviet navies escorted vessels through the strait. In 2019, Iran seized tankers and the U.S. responded with a naval presence. But those were near-misses. This is a formal closure — a different category of escalation. The difference between threatening to block it and actually blocking it is the difference between a risk premium and a real supply shock. Markets are now pricing the latter.

For Zubin specifically, this has several layers worth tracking. First, your GLD position: gold thrives in exactly this environment — fear, inflation, dollar uncertainty. Second, NLR (nuclear energy ETF): if oil at $108 stays sticky and policy-makers need a stable baseload alternative, nuclear gets a serious political tailwind. Third, your growth tech names (MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN) face margin pressure from higher energy costs at data centers and logistics networks. This isn't a sell call — but it's worth knowing why they might underperform oil and commodities in Q2.

The career angle is less obvious but real: the sports and entertainment industry runs on discretionary spending. When gas prices spike and household budgets compress, the first thing consumers cut is entertainment. Live event attendance, streaming subscriptions, ad budgets — all of these face headwinds in a sustained energy shock. That's the macro environment you'll be entering the industry in. Sports organizations that have built premium, experience-based revenue (think team ownership, luxury boxes, sponsorships tied to brand rather than ad spend) weather this better than pure media plays. Worth understanding the business model distinctions as you think about where to land.
