Ceasefire

Pakistan Brokers U.S.-Iran Deal — Two Weeks, Islamabad Talks Friday, Oil Crashes 15% to $94

President Trump suspended strikes on Iran with less than two hours to spare before his own deadline — and it was Pakistan that closed the deal. The terms require Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz for safe commercial passage during the truce. Markets reacted immediately: S&P 500 futures surged 2.5%, Dow futures added 1,056 points, WTI crude crashed more than 15% to $94.75 a barrel — the largest single-day oil drop in years. Travel stocks jumped 7%, airlines ripped, and European equities opened up 4% across the board. Pakistan's Prime Minister has invited U.S. and Iranian delegations to Islamabad this Friday, April 10, for continued talks to 'settle all disputes.'

The contradiction at the core of this deal is still there. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed Tehran will 'cease defensive operations' — but Iran simultaneously insists it will continue controlling Hormuz passage and enriching uranium. Gulf air defenses were still responding to incoming drones and missiles hours after the announcement. Shell's Q1 results — the first major energy company to report since the conflict — showed LNG production falling from 948K to 880-920K BOE due to war disruption, even as oil trading revenue surged. The ceasefire removes the tail risk that had markets pricing $120-130 oil. It doesn't remove the structural instability. Two weeks. Watch Islamabad Friday and watch Gulf air defense reports daily.

Strait of Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz Is 21 Miles Wide — Pakistan Just Brokered a Deal to Reopen It

Roughly 20% of the world's oil — and about 25% of its liquefied natural gas — passes through a narrow chokepoint between Iran and Oman called the Strait of Hormuz. At its tightest point, the navigable shipping lane is just 2 miles wide in each direction. When Trump threatened to destroy Iranian infrastructure unless Iran reopened the Strait, and markets responded by sending oil to $110+, that wasn't hysteria — it was a rational pricing of what a closed Strait actually means: immediate supply shock, spiking gasoline prices, aviation fuel chaos, and within weeks, real GDP contraction across oil-import-dependent economies. Last night Pakistan's Prime Minister stepped in and brokered the ceasefire. The Islamabad talks scheduled for Friday, April 10 will determine whether this becomes something more than a two-week pause.

Iran has threatened to close the Strait before — most recently in 2012, and during various U.S.-Iran tension episodes since. They've never actually done it, because closing it hurts Iran too: the country's own oil exports flow through the same channel. But 'never done it before' is cold comfort when Trump is posting 'whole civilization' threats on Truth Social. The real risk isn't Iran formally closing the Strait — it's that they make transit sufficiently dangerous (mines, missile threats, tanker harassment) that insurers won't cover shipping and commercial traffic stops voluntarily. Iran's FM confirmed today they will keep charging tolls and enriching uranium. That's not de-escalation. That's a managed pause.

For the portfolio: the ceasefire removes the tail risk that had traders hedging $120-130 oil. NLR (nuclear) and XLU (utilities), which rallied as petroleum alternatives during the conflict, may see pullback as crude eases. Airlines, travel, and logistics names rip today — a $20/barrel crude drop is a massive margin tailwind. The two-week clock is running. Watch the Islamabad talks Friday and watch whether Gulf air defense activity actually quiets down. This is intermission, not resolution.
