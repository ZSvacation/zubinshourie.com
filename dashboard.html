Today’s Rabbit Hole

Stagflation Is Back on the Table. Here’s What That Actually Means and Why It’s So Hard to Fight.

Stagflation — the combination of elevated inflation and weak or contracting growth — is the central bank’s worst nightmare because the normal tools work against each other. Raise rates to fight inflation, you slow growth and risk tipping into recession. Cut rates to stimulate, you pour fuel on inflation. The Fed ends up doing nothing while both problems get worse.

The last real episode was the 1970s–early ‘80s, driven by the OPEC oil embargo. The parallels to today are uncomfortable: a geopolitical energy shock (Hormuz instead of OPEC), supply-chain inflation compounding from outside the central bank’s control, and a labor market that was softening before the shock hit (February payrolls were -92K). The Fed held rates at 3.5–3.75% this week. Seven members project no cuts this year. Inflation is at 2.7% and rising. Growth is decelerating. Bank of England held too — same logic, different continent.

The variable no one can model is how long the Hormuz disruption lasts. If it ends in weeks, oil retreats, the energy shock is genuinely transitory, and the Fed threads the needle. If it grinds into summer, $115 Brent becomes $130, CPI re-accelerates, and the rate path gets rewritten again. Every $10 on a barrel of crude is roughly 0.2pp of CPI. Do the math on where we are vs. where we were in January.
