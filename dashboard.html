Iran Ceasefire

Trump Proposes 15-Point Peace Plan with Iran as Oil Slides Below $90

President Trump sent Iran a proposed one-month ceasefire framework with 15 conditions, and markets moved fast — Brent crude dropped from $110 earlier this week to around $98, WTI slid to $87.72, and U.S. equity futures surged over 1% in pre-market trading. The diplomatic gambit follows nearly four weeks of conflict that's hammered energy supply from the Gulf.

The economic collateral damage is already visible in the data: S&P Global's business surveys across the U.S., Europe, and Japan all flagged rising inflation expectations and slowing activity in March, with Europe's chief economist citing 'stagflation alarm bells.' Developing nations — Pakistan, Bangladesh, Egypt — are getting hit hardest, caught between surging import costs and depreciating currencies against the dollar.

If ceasefire talks gain traction, the energy relief trade could extend significantly — but $87 WTI is still elevated enough to keep inflation sticky. Watch Fed speakers this week for any updated tone on rate path in response to the oil shock.

Developing Nations Take the Brunt: Bangladesh Jet Fuel Up 111% Since Conflict Began

Countries across Asia, Africa, and the Middle East are absorbing energy price shocks with far less cushion than Western economies. Bangladesh raised jet fuel prices 79% in a single move — it's now up 111% since the Iran conflict started. The ADB has put emergency support packages on the table for member nations, but currency depreciation is compounding the damage.

AI vs. Rights Deals

AI Is Breaking the Old Sports Media Rights Model — And Nobody's Figured Out What Comes Next

For decades, sports media rights worked like a simple auction: networks bid, leagues won, and fans watched on cable. That model generated billions and seemed invincible. But AI is rewriting the underlying economics so fast that the traditional broadcast model is becoming structurally obsolete — and 2026 is the year the league offices are actually starting to feel it.

The shift isn't just about streaming replacing cable (that's old news). It's about what comes after streaming. Amazon's Prime Vision and the NBA-AWS partnership are already delivering real-time AI-generated tactical overlays, personalized highlight reels, and viewer-specific content feeds. A 25-year-old in Denver and a 60-year-old in Phoenix can watch the same game and see completely different broadcast experiences — different stats, different camera angles, different ads. The value of a 'broadcast rights deal' in that world is fundamentally different from what it was in 2010.

For someone entering sports marketing and entertainment, this is probably the most important structural shift to understand right now. The athlete-as-media-platform trend (NIL plus AI) means athletes can build direct-to-fan distribution that bypasses league structures entirely. Meanwhile, leagues are renegotiating how they think about rights — moving toward equity partnerships with distributors rather than straight cash deals. The people who understand how AI personalizes content and monetizes fan data at scale will be building the next generation of sports media companies.

What to watch: The next NBA national TV deal cycle, how the NFL structures its streaming rights post-2033, and whether any major sport breaks away from traditional network deals entirely in favor of a direct streaming model. The money is enormous and the playbook hasn't been written yet.
