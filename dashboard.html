Oil Shock

Iran War Drives Biggest Oil Supply Disruption in History — Brent Above $105, Global Growth Revised Down

The war in Iran has now triggered what analysts are calling the largest oil supply disruption in the history of the global oil market — a loss of approximately 20 million barrels per day. Brent crude has crossed $105/barrel as U.S. and Israeli strikes on Persian Gulf energy infrastructure continue, and the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to normal shipping traffic.

The ripple effects go well beyond the pump. Fertilizer prices are spiking (a significant chunk of global nitrogen exports transits Hormuz), semiconductor manufacturing faces helium shortages, and developing nations are rationing fuel. The OECD has revised global GDP growth down to 2.9% for 2026 — wiping out gains that had been baked in from AI spending and easing tariffs. The IMF is projecting an additional 0.5pp of global inflation on top of existing pressures.

For markets, the calculus is brutal: central banks globally — Fed, ECB, BoE, BoJ — are caught between energy-driven inflation and slowing growth. Rate cuts are off the table in most scenarios; rate hikes are back in play for some. Equity markets finished Friday lower across the board. Your energy and utility holdings (NLR, XLU) are the obvious beneficiaries in this environment.

The Agent Economy

AI Agents Are About to Hollow Out Mid-Level Sports and Entertainment Jobs — Here's What That Means If You're Entering the Industry in 2026

The agentic AI wave isn't coming — it's here. This month alone, autonomous AI platforms launched that can handle email management, scheduling, content creation, research, and software development without human hand-holding. The global agentic AI market is projected to exceed $10.9 billion this year. In sports and entertainment, the disruption is landing hardest in exactly the entry-to-mid-level functions that new graduates have historically used to build careers: scouting reports, PR outreach, social content, sponsorship decks, data analysis.

What's changing isn't just efficiency — it's the labor model. Teams and agencies are experimenting with AI agents that can generate 100 personalized fan marketing emails in the time it takes a human to draft one. Sports media companies are using AI to auto-generate game summaries, highlight packages, and even basic broadcast commentary. Talent agencies are deploying AI for contract research and comp analysis. The $6.6 billion AI sports market projection for 2026 isn't coming from hardware — it's coming from workflow automation that displaces headcount.

The so-what for you specifically: the jobs that survive automation are the ones requiring genuine relationship capital, creative judgment, and contextual taste — things AI can simulate but not replace. A PR person who can read a room, a marketing director who understands brand voice at a cultural level, a talent rep who has real relationships — those roles are getting more valuable, not less. The danger is the pipeline narrows: fewer entry-level jobs means fewer people developing the skills to do the senior work. Firms that figure out how to use AI to multiply one sharp human rather than replace ten mediocre ones will win.

What to watch: which sports properties and agencies are the early adopters of agentic workflows, and which are resisting. The adopters will look lean and efficient for 2-3 years, then potentially face a talent crisis when they need experienced people and haven't grown any. The resisters will look expensive and slow, then catch up fast when they do move. For someone entering the industry: get fluent with these tools now. Being the person who understands both the business and the AI is the moat.
