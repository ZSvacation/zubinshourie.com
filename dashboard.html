Hormuz Deadline

Trump Sets Final Ultimatum: Iran Has Until Tuesday 8PM ET to Reopen the Strait or Face 'Devastating Consequences'

The clock is running. President Trump has set an April 7 deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — effectively closed since joint US-Israeli airstrikes hit Iran on February 28. If Iran doesn't comply, Trump has threatened strikes on power plants and bridges, warning Tehran it will be 'living in Hell.' Airstrikes over the weekend already killed more than 25 people inside Iran.

The Strait carries roughly one-fifth of global oil flow. Its closure has pushed WTI crude to $113.62 and Brent past $110, with gas prices topping $6 in parts of the US. Mediators from Pakistan, Turkey, and Egypt are attempting to broker a 45-day ceasefire, but Iran says it won't reopen the waterway for a temporary deal — and has signaled the Strait has undergone 'irreversible changes.'

Markets are holding their breath. Equities are up modestly in pre-market — S&P futures +0.35%, Nasdaq +0.39% — but that's largely 'deal optimism' baked in. If no resolution emerges by tomorrow night, the energy shock deepens fast. GLD, XLU, and NLR in Zubin's portfolio all have direct exposure to this outcome.

Today’s Rabbit Hole
Geopolitics · Energy Markets · Infrastructure
Strait Chokepoint

The Strait of Hormuz Is Closed — Here's What That Actually Means for the Global Economy (and Your Portfolio)

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. Through those 21 miles flows roughly 20% of the world's oil supply and 17% of global LNG — about 20 million barrels per day. It is the single most consequential maritime chokepoint on Earth, and as of today, it has been effectively closed to US, Israeli, and allied vessels for over five weeks. This isn't a theoretical tail risk anymore. It's happening.

The economic mechanics are straightforward but the scale is staggering. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, and Qatar all export through the Strait. Saudi's Yanbu pipeline on the Red Sea can handle roughly 5 million barrels/day as an alternative — a fraction of what normally flows. There is no spare capacity that can compensate for full Hormuz closure. The result: every barrel of oil in global circulation just became more scarce, more expensive to route, and more uncertain in delivery. That's what $113 WTI is pricing. The Fed cannot cut into this — lower rates accelerate inflation — which is why the central bank is frozen despite a weakening labor market. Stagflation isn't a prediction anymore; it's the current operating environment.

For Zubin's portfolio specifically: GLD benefits from uncertainty and inflation (traditional safe haven); XLU and NLR (nuclear/utilities) benefit as energy alternatives become more attractive; VXUS gives international diversification but comes with significant ASEAN exposure. On the risk side, high-multiple tech and growth names (PLTR, CRWV, APLD) are somewhat insulated from energy prices but face a higher-for-longer rate environment that compresses valuations. SOFI and consumer-facing fintech face the most direct headwind from a softening, inflation-squeezed consumer.

The 48-hour watch: Trump's April 7 deadline is the pivot point. Three scenarios — (1) Iran blinks and reopens, oil craters $15-20, equities rally hard; (2) ceasefire deal buys time, oil stays range-bound ~$100-105, market cautiously exhales; (3) no deal and US escalates militarily, oil spikes toward $130+, equities sell off aggressively. Scenario 2 is what the market is currently pricing. Watch the TBS broadcast tonight, but keep one eye on Truth Social.
