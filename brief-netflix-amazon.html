Netflix Taps Amazon's Shopping Data — And Streaming Just Became a Performance Advertising Channel

Marketing Dive · AdWeek · PPC Land · About Netflix

On March 4, 2026, Netflix announced it was integrating Amazon's audience targeting capabilities into its advertising platform — along with Yahoo ConnectID and, notably, its own first-party Conversion API. For most of its advertising history, Netflix has been a brand awareness play: you buy it to get your logo in front of affluent, engaged audiences, and you accept that you can't really measure what happens after. That's changing, and the change is more significant than it might sound on the surface.

What Amazon Brings to the Table

Amazon's DSP (Demand-Side Platform) is built on what the company describes as "trillions of proprietary shopping, streaming, and browsing signals." When someone buys a camera on Amazon, returns running shoes twice a year, or streams cooking content regularly — Amazon knows that. That behavioral purchase data is now available to inform targeting on Netflix inventory. In practical terms, it means advertisers can reach a Netflix audience segment like "people who recently purchased athletic gear" or "users actively browsing travel" — behavioral signals that go far beyond traditional demographic or interest targeting.

"Early campaigns using Netflix's new Conversion API outperformed benchmarks by more than 75% across financial services, edtech, and retail clients." — Tinuiti

The Conversion API Changes the Value Proposition Entirely

The more significant development for performance marketers is Netflix's launch of its own Conversion API (CAPI). A Conversion API lets advertisers pass purchase and conversion data from their own systems back to the ad platform — the same mechanism that saved Meta's ad business after Apple's App Tracking Transparency changes killed cookie-based attribution. Netflix launching its own CAPI signals that it's now trying to make streaming measurably attributable: you can start to answer the question "did someone who saw my ad on Netflix actually buy something?"

Early results from Tinuiti — which has tested the integration with financial services, edtech, and retail clients — showed campaigns outperforming benchmarks by more than 75%. That number will need more validation at scale, but the directional signal is clear: Netflix is no longer just a reach play. It's building the infrastructure of a full-funnel performance channel.

Why This Matters for the Industry

Netflix's advertising revenue crossed $1.5 billion in 2025 and the company has projected it will roughly double to $3 billion in 2026. That growth trajectory requires convincing performance-oriented advertisers — who want attribution, ROI, and accountability — to move dollars in from search and social. The Amazon and Conversion API integrations are exactly the infrastructure those advertisers need to justify the spend. Combined with Yahoo ConnectID's 232 million logged-in US users for identity matching, Netflix has quietly built something that looks a lot more like a media platform than a streaming service.
