One Piece Season 2, Scarpetta, and the Jason Bateman Thing on HBO

The Daily Brief · March 11, 2026

Three significant releases dropped this week across Netflix and HBO, none of which are particularly similar in tone or audience. Here's the rundown.

One Piece Season 2 — Netflix

Season 1 of the live-action One Piece was the rare manga adaptation that actually worked — it held the tone of the source material without getting precious about it, and the casting was mostly excellent. Season 2 picks up with the Alabasta arc, widely considered one of the strongest in the original series, following Luffy and the crew as they help a desert princess take back her kingdom from a crime syndicate led by one of the Seven Warlords of the Sea.

The casting news for Season 2 has been the main talking point pre-release: Daniel Radcliffe plays Sir Crocodile, the primary antagonist. It's a genuinely unexpected piece of casting — Crocodile is a cold, calculating villain with a lot of physical presence, and Radcliffe's persona runs the opposite direction. Early reactions say it works, which is either surprising or isn't, depending on your read on Radcliffe's post-Potter career.

If you watched Season 1 and liked it, Season 2 is reportedly better. If you didn't watch Season 1, this is not the entry point.

Scarpetta — Netflix

Nicole Kidman plays forensic pathologist Kay Scarpetta, Patricia Cornwell's long-running literary protagonist, in this eight-episode adaptation. The character is one of the most popular in crime fiction — Cornwell has published 25 novels featuring Scarpetta since 1990 — but she's never had a definitive screen adaptation, which makes this the most anticipated version. Kidman is well-cast: the character is a meticulous, emotionally guarded professional who uses work as a coping mechanism, and Kidman has spent her career playing exactly that type.

The show picks up mid-career, with Scarpetta working a high-profile case in New York while managing a complicated dynamic with FBI agent Pete Marino (Edgar Ramirez) and her niece Lucy (Sadie Sink), who has become a cybersecurity expert. Early reviews are strong, calling it the best crime procedural Netflix has released since Mindhunter.

The Thing on HBO (Untitled — Working Title: "Triangle")

HBO dropped a dark comedy thriller starring Jason Bateman, David Harbour, and Linda Cardellini as three friends whose relationship turns lethal. The show is structured like a procedural but plays more like a dark comedy — the premise involves a love triangle that ends in murder, with the investigation told from rotating perspectives. Think Fleabag meets Fargo in tone, which either sounds perfect or insufferable depending on your tastes.

Bateman and Harbour are in well-worn territory (both have done this kind of morally compromised character before), but Cardellini is doing something genuinely different from her usual range. Worth watching the first two episodes before deciding.
