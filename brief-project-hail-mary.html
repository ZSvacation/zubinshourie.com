Project Hail Mary — Ryan Gosling Wakes Up Alone in Space and Has to Save the World

Amazon MGM Studios · Directed by Phil Lord & Christopher Miller

Ryan Gosling plays Ryland Grace, a junior high school science teacher who wakes up on a spacecraft with no memory of who he is, how he got there, or what he's supposed to be doing. As his memory slowly returns, the picture gets both clearer and more terrifying: the sun is dying. A mysterious substance called Astrophage is draining its energy, and Grace was sent on a one-way solo mission — the human race's last idea — to figure out how to stop it before Earth freezes over. No pressure.

Why It's Getting Attention

The film is adapted from Andy Weir's 2021 novel of the same name, which was widely regarded as one of the best science fiction books in years. Weir previously wrote The Martian, and Project Hail Mary shares its DNA: a lone scientist using first-principles thinking to solve impossible problems, written with genuine scientific rigor and deadpan humor. The film is directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller — the duo behind The Lego Movie and the 21 Jump Street franchise — which signals they're leaning into the book's tonal balance of dread and comedy rather than playing it as pure prestige drama.

"96% on Rotten Tomatoes after its London premiere. First reactions are calling it the best science fiction film since Arrival."

Gosling is doing what he's increasingly good at: playing someone who is technically extremely competent but emotionally adrift, using intellect as a coping mechanism for circumstances that are genuinely overwhelming. The film was shot with IMAX cameras, meaning the space sequences are built to be experienced at full scale. It opens wide March 20 via Amazon MGM Studios.

The Relationship at the Center

The book's most beloved element — and what early reviewers are praising in the film — is the unexpected friendship that develops roughly a third of the way through the story. Without spoiling it, Grace encounters another entity in deep space that shouldn't be there, and the way their relationship develops is both genuinely funny and unexpectedly moving. It's the emotional core that elevates the film beyond a puzzle-solving survival story. If you've read the book, you know. If you haven't, go in without looking it up — it's better that way.
